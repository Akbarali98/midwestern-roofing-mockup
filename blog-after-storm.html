After the Storm: A Homeowner's Guide to Roof Damage and Insurance Claims

March 28, 2026
5 min read
By Midwestern Construction Team

Northeast Ohio's weather doesn't pull punches. One afternoon you're enjoying a blue sky, and by evening a line of severe thunderstorms or a hail-dropping system has rolled through Akron and left homeowners wondering what just happened to their roofs. At Midwestern Construction, we're a family-owned company built on honesty and integrity — and part of that means being a steady hand when things are stressful. Here's what to do after a storm, step by step.

How to Spot Potential Storm Damage From the Ground

Before you call anyone, take a few minutes to do a safe ground-level inspection. You don't need to get on the roof yourself — in fact, we'd prefer you didn't. Here's what to look for from the yard and gutter line:

  • Missing or damaged shingles — Look for visible gaps, lifted edges, cracked sections, or shingles that have slid out of place.
  • Granule loss — Check your gutters and downspout splash zones. A significant amount of dark, sand-like granules is a sign that hail or wind has scoured your shingles.
  • Dents and dings on metal — Gutters, flashing, and vents are good hail indicators. If your aluminum gutters have circular dents, your shingles likely took a beating too.
  • Fallen branches or debris — Even if a branch didn't punch through, impact can crack decking or crack shingles in ways not visible from the ground.
  • Interior signs — Water stains on ceilings or walls after a storm mean water is already getting in somewhere.

"If you're not sure whether you have damage, call us for a free inspection. The worst outcome of calling is peace of mind. The worst outcome of not calling is a small leak becoming a major structural problem."

Your First Steps: What to Do Immediately After a Storm

Time matters after storm damage, both for your home's protection and for your insurance claim. Here's the order of operations:

  1. Document everything before anything moves. Use your phone to photograph and video your roof from the ground, your gutters, any visible interior damage, and your yard. Date and timestamp everything. This documentation is critical for your insurance claim.
  2. Prevent further damage safely. If you have an active leak, use buckets to collect water and place towels to protect flooring. Do not attempt to tarp your roof yourself — wet roofs during or after storms are extremely dangerous. We can handle emergency tarping.
  3. Call a trusted local roofer before your insurance company. We know this sounds counterintuitive, but here's why: a professional assessment from a contractor you trust gives you an independent picture of the damage before an insurance adjuster — who works for the insurance company — does their evaluation. This protects you.

Navigating the Insurance Claim Process with Confidence

Once you have a professional assessment in hand, you're ready to file your claim. Here's what to expect:

Filing the Claim

Contact your homeowner's insurance carrier and file a claim for storm damage. They'll assign an adjuster to inspect your property. Ask for a specific appointment time and request that your contractor be present during the adjuster's visit — a good contractor will know what the adjuster is looking for and can make sure nothing is missed.

The Adjuster Visit

Your adjuster will assess the damage and determine whether it meets your deductible threshold and policy terms. Having your roofing contractor present as an advocate is one of the most valuable things you can do. We've seen legitimate damage overlooked because no one was there to point it out.

Understanding Your Settlement

Your insurance company will issue a scope of loss document and a check for the actual cash value (ACV) of repairs, minus your deductible. If your policy includes replacement cost value (RCV) coverage, a second check is typically issued after the work is completed. Read every line and ask questions — we're happy to help you interpret the paperwork.

Don't Forget Your Gutters

Hail and windblown debris are hard on gutters. Dented or separated gutters can't channel water away from your foundation properly, leading to basement flooding, foundation erosion, and — in winter — ice dams that push water back under your shingles. Make sure any comprehensive storm damage assessment includes a full gutter inspection.
